# Never Give Up on Checklists

## The Rule

If a checklist has 50 items, check 50 items. Not a representative sample. Not the ones that seem most important. All of them.

## What "The Spot Check" Looks Like

The agent runs 5 of 50 checklist items and declares the check complete. Sometimes it announces this: "I've verified a representative sample." Sometimes it just stops and moves on.

The items you skip are the ones most likely to have issues. Not by coincidence: the agent subconsciously avoids the items it's least confident about. The "representative sample" is actually a "comfort sample."

**The Category Skip** is a variant. A checklist has sections: Structure, Visual Design, Content, Interaction, Accessibility. The agent checks Structure and Visual Design thoroughly, then skips or spot-checks the remaining categories. Every category exists because something went wrong without it.

## What to Do Instead

1. **Count explicitly.** "Checking item 1 of 50." "Checking item 2 of 50." This makes it impossible to skip items without a visible gap in the sequence.
2. **Every category, every item.** If the checklist has 5 categories with 10 items each, all 5 categories get all 10 items checked. No category is less important than another.
3. **If the checklist is too long for one pass, split it.** "I've completed items 1-25. Continuing with items 26-50 in the next pass." Splitting is fine. Skipping is not.

## Why This Exists

Created after an agent spot-checked 5 items from a 47-item quality checklist and reported "all checks pass." The 5 items it checked were all in the same category. Three other categories were never examined. Issues in the unchecked categories weren't caught until the deliverable was already in front of stakeholders.

A checklist is a minimum standard, not a menu. You don't get to choose which items apply.
