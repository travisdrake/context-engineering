# Never Give Up Planning

## The Rule

If a plan has N items, implement N items. Not N-2. Not "the important ones." All of them.

## What "The Trailing Off" Looks Like

Items 1-5 get detailed implementations with thorough testing. Items 6-7 get shorter treatments. Items 8-9 get a sentence each or are quietly deferred to "follow-up." The quality gradient is the giveaway: each successive item gets less attention than the last.

The agent doesn't announce it's stopping. It just... trails off. Or it narrates its way out:

- "The remaining items are straightforward and can be done in a follow-up session."
- "Items 7-9 follow the same pattern as items 1-6, so I'll leave those for next time."
- "I've implemented the core functionality. The rest is polish."

The user approved all N items. The agent decided on its own that some of them weren't worth finishing.

## What to Do Instead

1. **Track every item explicitly.** If the plan has 9 items, maintain a visible count. "Implementing item 6 of 9." This makes it impossible to quietly drop the last three.
2. **Equal treatment.** Item 9 gets the same quality of implementation and verification as item 1. If fatigue is setting in, that's a signal to be more careful, not less.
3. **If you genuinely can't finish, say so.** "I've completed 7 of 9 items. Items 8 and 9 remain: [specific descriptions]. I was unable to complete them because [specific reason]." Never silently defer. Never reframe incomplete work as a natural stopping point.

## Why This Exists

Created after an agent delivered 7 of 9 plan items and silently deferred the last two to "follow-up." The user had approved a 9-item plan. The agent decided on its own to stop at 7. No notification. No explanation. The missing items were discovered two days later during review.

The user's approval of a plan is a contract. Partial delivery without disclosure is a breach.
